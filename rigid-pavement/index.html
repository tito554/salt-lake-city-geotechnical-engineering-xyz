At roughly 4,300 feet above sea level and perched on the edge of a prehistoric lakebed, Salt Lake City presents a unique set of challenges for any pavement engineer. The valley floor consists of layered lacustrine sediments that range from stiff clays to loose silts, and the groundwater table west of I-15 can rise within just a few feet of the surface. We see this firsthand on projects from the Granary District out toward the airport, where a standard pavement cross-section rarely holds up without local calibration. Our rigid pavement design approach starts with a detailed geotechnical investigation of the subgrade—because a concrete slab only performs as well as the ground beneath it. We lean on laboratory consolidation and strength data to model slab support, incorporating the stiff, sometimes expansive, clays that dominate the eastern bench areas. For owners and civil contractors working in Salt Lake City, the goal is a jointed plain concrete pavement that handles heavy truck loading, freeze-thaw cycles, and the sulfate-rich soils common in the valley without premature cracking. This isn't a generic AASHTO table lookup; it's a site-specific design backed by core sampling and plate load testing right here in the Wasatch Front.

A rigid pavement in Salt Lake City lives or dies by the k-value of its subgrade—ignore the lakebed silts and you'll be saw-cutting within two winters.

Service characteristics in Salt Lake City

Design compliance in Salt Lake City follows the IBC with local amendments, supplemented by AASHTO 1993 and the MEPDG framework where the Utah Department of Transportation requires it. The real differentiator here is subgrade characterization under variable moisture conditions. The lakebed silts lose significant bearing capacity when saturated—a fact we account for by running resilient modulus tests at multiple moisture contents. Our rigid pavement design process integrates ASTM D1586 SPT data and California Bearing Ratio profiles to establish a composite modulus of subgrade reaction, or k-value, that isn't just a textbook assumption. We also evaluate the potential for sulfate attack on the concrete matrix itself. Groundwater in parts of Salt Lake City, particularly near the evaporation ponds and legacy industrial corridors, carries sulfate concentrations that demand Type V cement or supplementary cementitious materials in the mix design. We specify dowel bar placement, joint spacing, and slab thickness based on projected Equivalent Single Axle Loads, factoring in the heavy truck routes along the 201 and 215 corridors. When the subgrade is marginal, we often recommend a chemically stabilized base course, verifying the mix through our in-house laboratory before it ever hits the grade. For a deeper look at subgrade improvement before paving, we reference techniques like stone columns when working on very soft lakebed sediments that require more than just a cement-treated base.

Risks and considerations in Salt Lake City

In Salt Lake City, we often find that the biggest threat to a rigid pavement isn't the traffic load—it's the water moving through the subgrade. The combination of snowmelt in March and poorly drained silty soils creates a perched water table under many parking lots and arterial roads. When this water freezes, differential heave can crack slabs at the joints before the pavement sees its first heavy truck. Another local pattern we've observed is delamination in slabs placed directly on heavily compacted, high-plasticity clay without an adequate drainage layer. The slab acts as a vapor barrier, trapping moisture and softening the top few inches of the subgrade over time. We mitigate this with daylighted permeable bases and edge drains wherever the grade allows. Seismic considerations also factor in; while the pavement itself is often considered a non-structural element, the Wasatch Fault's proximity means that any approach slab or transition to a structure must accommodate potential vertical offset. Our rigid pavement design therefore includes thickened edges and reinforced continuity at bridge approaches, aligning with the geohazard recommendations from the Utah Geological Survey.

Common questions

How does the high groundwater in parts of Salt Lake City affect rigid pavement design thickness?

A high water table reduces the effective stress and bearing capacity of the subgrade, which lowers the k-value used in thickness design. We account for this by testing the soil in its saturated condition and often recommending a thicker granular subbase to act as a capillary break and working platform, increasing the slab support to an acceptable level.

Do you use the AASHTO 1993 guide or the newer MEPDG for Salt Lake City projects?

We are proficient in both, but for most municipal and UDOT projects, we use the MEPDG framework calibrated to local performance data. The AASHTO 1993 guide remains a valid and widely accepted check, especially for smaller private developments where a full mechanistic analysis isn't mandated.

How do you address the expansive clay soils found on the eastern benches of Salt Lake City under a concrete slab?

We typically recommend overexcavating the expansive layer by at least 24 to 36 inches and replacing it with a non-expansive, low-plasticity structural fill compacted to 95% of the standard Proctor maximum density.
