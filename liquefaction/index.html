Salt Lake City sits at 4,265 feet above sea level, right against the active Wasatch Fault zone. The valley floor isn't bedrock—it's hundreds of feet of soft, saturated lacustrine sediment from ancient Lake Bonneville. When the 2020 Magna earthquake shook the region at a magnitude 5.7, it reminded every structural engineer here how critical a proper liquefaction analysis is before breaking ground. The fine silts and sands that make up much of the downtown and western corridor are exactly the type of material that loses strength under cyclic loading. A standard bearing capacity check won't catch it. You need site-specific data: SPT blow counts, fines content from a grain size analysis, and groundwater depth measured in real conditions. Our team integrates field data with lab testing under ASTM D1586 and D2487 to build a defensible liquefaction potential index for your site. No guesswork—just numbers that hold up to plan review.

Service characteristics in Salt Lake City

The lakebed sediments across Salt Lake City don't behave uniformly. In the Avenues foothills you might hit coarse alluvial fans, but drive three miles west toward the airport and the profile shifts to interbedded clays and loose silty sands with groundwater at six feet. That contrast demands a testing program that captures spatial variability. We run SPT borings at multiple locations and depths, measure in-situ density, and pair it with laboratory Atterberg limits and grain size curves. For critical structures—think data centers in West Valley or medical facilities near the University of Utah—we add CPT soundings for a continuous profile of tip resistance and sleeve friction. The liquefaction triggering analysis follows the simplified procedure outlined in ASCE 7-22, with site class determined per IBC Chapter 16. Post-liquefaction settlement and lateral spreading displacement are quantified so your structural team knows what deformation to design for. Risks and considerations in Salt Lake City

We run a truck-mounted CME-75 drill rig through the wide streets of Salt Lake City, pulling continuous split-spoon samples where the water table sits high in the old lakebed. The risk with a superficial desk study is that it misses a thin loose layer sandwiched between stiffer strata—and that's exactly the layer that will liquefy and cause differential settlement. Soil liquefaction analysis isn't just about a yes/no trigger; it's about quantifying settlement in inches and lateral spread in feet. A project in the Glendale neighborhood needs different mitigation parameters than one on the East Bench. We've seen sites where LPI values shift from moderate to high just by adjusting the groundwater assumption to seasonal maximum. That's why we install a PVC monitoring well and record water level at 24 hours and again at one week. Ground truth matters more than any empirical correlation.

Our services

Our liquefaction investigation package moves from field exploration to lab verification to final analysis under one scope of work. Every project gets a site-specific report with LPI maps, settlement estimates, and mitigation recommendations ready for structural design review.

SPT-Based Liquefaction Assessment

We drill boreholes to depths of 30 to 50 feet, recording blow counts every 2.5 feet in the critical zone. Samples are sealed and transported to our lab for fines content, plasticity, and moisture testing. The liquefaction triggering analysis uses the Boulanger & Idriss (2014) CPT and SPT-based procedures, comparing CSR to CRR at each depth with a factor of safety output.

CPT Sounding and Seismic Integration

For high-value structures in Salt Lake City’s soft lakebed, we push a seismic CPT cone to capture tip resistance, sleeve friction, and pore pressure continuously. Shear wave velocity data refines the Site Class determination and allows a more precise CRR calculation, reducing conservatism and potentially lowering foundation costs.

Common questions

What triggers a liquefaction analysis requirement in Salt Lake City?

The IBC mandates a liquefaction study when the mapped spectral acceleration at 1-second period exceeds certain thresholds and groundwater is within 50 feet of grade. Most of the Salt Lake Valley floor, classified as Site Class D or E by default, exceeds these thresholds. Any structure assigned to Risk Category III or IV—schools, hospitals, emergency response facilities—will almost always require a site-specific liquefaction analysis per ASCE 7.

How deep do boreholes need to go for a liquefaction study here?

Borehole depth depends on the stratigraphy but typically reaches 30 to 50 feet in the Salt Lake Valley. The key is to penetrate through the Lake Bonneville sand and silt layers into the stiffer, older deposits below. We log every boring with continuous SPT sampling in the upper 30 feet, where liquefaction is most likely. If deeper loose layers are encountered, we extend the boring until competent material is confirmed.

What does a liquefaction analysis cost for a typical commercial lot?

For a standard commercial parcel in Salt Lake City requiring two to three borings with SPT sampling, lab testing, and a full liquefaction report, budgets generally range from US$2,320 to US$4,340. The upper end applies when CPT soundings, seismic cone data, or deeper drilling is needed. Every quote is site-specific after reviewing the geotechnical conditions and project structural loads.

Can you mitigate liquefaction risk without deep foundations?

Yes, several ground improvement techniques can densify loose sands and silts to reduce liquefaction potential. Stone columns, vibrocompaction, and deep dynamic compaction are options we evaluate based on soil gradation and site access. In Salt Lake City’s dense urban areas, vibration-sensitive adjacent structures often make grouting or rigid inclusion methods more appropriate. We provide a comparative feasibility analysis in the final report.
